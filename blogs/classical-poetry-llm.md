# Writing classical poetry in the age of LLMs

Why I still carve verses by hand, even when AI can do it in seconds.

## The Temptation of Ease

Last month, I tried asking a large language model to write a *ci* poem in the style of Li Qingzhao. The result was... competent. The tonal patterns were correct, the imagery was appropriate, and it even managed to capture some of the melancholy that characterizes her later work.

And yet, something felt hollow.

## The Craft of Struggle

When I write a poem, I often spend hours on a single line. Take this couplet from a recent piece:

> 梧桐盡低語，相約赴黃昏。

The paulownia trees murmur softly to one another, making a quiet appointment to meet the dusk.

The word "低語" (whisper) — I must have tried five different verbs before settling on it. "輕語"? Too plain. "細語"? Too common. "低語" has that quality of intimacy, of secrets being shared.

## Why the Struggle Matters

Here's what AI cannot replicate: the *resistance* of language. When I wrestle with words late into the night, when I feel the frustration of almost-there but not-quite — that struggle is where meaning is forged.

The AI generates effortlessly. But effortlessness is not freedom. True freedom in poetry comes from mastering constraint, from choosing *this* word and not *that* word, and bearing full responsibility for the choice.

## A Personal Manifesto

So I continue to write by hand, to revise painfully, to feel the weight of each character. Not because AI cannot do it faster, but because the slowness itself is the point.

> 我亦如常日日醉，夜夜達歡至凌晨。
> 功名利益休縛我，地籟天籟送青春。

I drink as always, day after day lost in wine, revelling every night until the small hours come. Let fame and fortune leave me well alone — the music of earth and sky will see my youth out.

This poem took me three nights. An AI could have generated it in three seconds. But those three nights were mine.
